# Content

## Masterbrand Expression Principles



Content is the text-related part of an interface. Phrases, button texts, success and error messages are within the scope of Content. Below you will find guidelines to guide your decisions related to creating product content.

## Expression Principles

Our purpose, our values and our inventiveness materialize in four principles that guide our entire brand expression:

1. **Intentionally Simple**  
   Straight to the point, intuitive, with attention to every detail, making people's lives easier.  
   _E.g.: Accessible language, intuitive app experience, clean images._

2. **Warmly Interactive**  
   Immersive experiences, with lots of movement and dynamism, bring our communication to life.  
   _E.g.: Fully interactive website, focusing on video, moving illustrations._

3. **Spirited**  
   Captivating, yet insightful, more mature, making the world vibrate with our enthusiastic attitude.  
   _E.g.: Intelligent sense of humor in the texts, cheerful acting, touches of purple in the images, cultural references._

4. **Culturally Connected**  
   Genuinely in tune with cultural trends, following the modern taste and references of our target audience.  
   _E.g.: Diversity in the cast, modern filming locations, collaborations with creators._

> It's not always possible to apply the principles of expression. They are not an obligation but rather a guide of intention for our maximum expression.

## Expression Principles Into Tone of Voice

Translating our principles of expression into tone of voice, we behave this way:

### Intentionally Simple → Objective

> Objective: "That presents speed and practicality in actions; that does not get lost in speculations; direct, practical, positive."

Objective, in which each word fulfills an exact function. No more, no less.

### Warmly Interactive → Attentive

> Attentive: "Who pays attention. Delicate in words and attitudes; courteous, kind. Done with great care; thorough."

Attentive, using empathy to create closeness. After all, before being technological, we are human.

### Spirited → Spirited

> Spirited: "Which is intelligently entertaining; which is lively, subtle, precise and laughter-provoking."

Spirited, lively and interesting, but precise and subtle in the use of humor.

### Culturally Connected → Up to date

> Up to date: "Who is well informed, on top of events; attentive, connected."

Up to date, keeping your finger on the pulse of relevant events and incorporating elements with the purpose of creating connections.

## Tone of Voice Dimensions

### Intentionally Simple – **Objective**

Objective, in which each word fulfills an exact function. No more, no less.

- **Simple, direct and transparent. Short, no-gotcha phrases.**  
  Use short sentences and small paragraphs and be as direct as possible.  
  This makes reading more digestible and less intimidating, and reinforces our transparency.

- **Use words that are easy to understand.**  
  Choose easy words that are widely known and spoken by people.  
  They facilitate understanding and avoid misunderstandings.

- **Organize, focus on one point, use clear examples.**  
  Try to talk about one topic at a time and use clear examples and analogies.  
  They help illustrate your point and reinforce understanding.

- **Be careful not to be insensitive, cold or reductionist.**  
  In general, be careful that objectivity does not become insensitivity.  
  We aim to make people's lives easier and take up as little time as possible in their lives, but we are still human and empathetic.

---

### Warmly Interactive – **Attentive**

Attentive, using empathy to create closeness. After all, before being technological, we are human.

- **Use curiosity to create genuine connections.**  
  Encourage conversation by asking questions and showing interest.  
  This makes people feel more heard.

- **Use words that are easy to understand.**  
  Choose easy words that are widely known and spoken by people.  
  They facilitate understanding and avoid misunderstandings.

- **Organize, focus on one point, use clear examples.**  
  Try to talk about one topic at a time and use clear examples and analogies.  
  They help illustrate your point and reinforce understanding.

- **Be careful not to be intimate, forced or exaggerated.**  
  In general, be careful not to confuse attention with excessive intimacy.  
  We are human and friendly, but for people, we are still a financial institution, so we need to maintain the objectivity we talked about on the previous page.

---

### Spirited – **Spirited**

Spirited, lively and interesting, but precise and subtle in the use of humor.

- **Show charisma and good humor.**  
  Our expression is always vibrant and positive, like that of someone who wakes up without an alarm clock on a restful Saturday.  
  Our good mood is contagious around us.

- **Have the same references as your audience.**  
  Use your target references to connect in an interesting way.  
  This adds depth and insight to your text.

- **Make use of popular sayings and expressions.**  
  Adapt famous expressions that we have heard our whole lives to our reality.  
  They add color and creativity to the text, making reading more attractive.

- **Be careful not to appear unprofessional, inappropriate or childish.**  
  In general, we need to be careful not to overdo it and appear inadequate.  
  We are a mature brand, in which millions of people trust their finances.  
  We don't say anything to people that we wouldn't say in a professional, respectful work environment.  
  Remember: pay attention to the context.

---

### Culturally Connected – **Up to date**

Up to date, keeping your finger on the pulse of relevant events and incorporating elements with the purpose of creating connections.

- **Be observant of the social context and your audience.**  
  Pay attention to events and use your observations to connect.  
  This helps keep us always fresh and connected to what's happening.

- **We use emojis with purpose.**  
  We use emojis when they serve a relevant purpose: to help illustrate or complement the text in a way that could not be done so well by words.  
  This helps to mature the brand, but without making it old-fashioned.

- **We use popular expressions with purpose.**  
  We can adapt memes or even popular expressions to financial reality, in order to generate connection.  
  They add color and creativity to the text, making reading more attractive.

- **Be careful with exaggerations.**  
  We want to inspire confidence and professionalism, but without losing our youthfulness, so recognized by our customers.  
  Therefore, we must think that the use of emojis and memes should be in 10% of our communications.  
  Choose to use this 10% in situations where it will actually have a relevant purpose of generating genuine connections.

## Content Heuristics

### Writing for Everyone

Our content must reach as many people as possible and provide the opportunity for all of these people to understand the guidelines, explanations and actions to be taken.

To meet such criteria, we must take into account the level of reading and readability of the texts. A standard established by Unesco puts simple and clear language as a way to meet the world's basic educational level of people.

#### Tips

- If the sentence is long, try to break it into two smaller sentences.
- Avoid repeating words and redundant words.
- Use conversational language and with references to the spoken language.
- Avoid technical and far-fetched terms whenever possible.
- Test your content whenever you can.

#### Tools

Here we suggest two tools to help you follow some content assumptions:

- <https://hemingwayapp.com/>  
  Copy and paste your text and see the readability score. Listed in a mathematical formula that measures number of words, syllables and sentence length. Look for a score of 6 or less.
- <https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1PhPaYr2DMj1FG4hi6g55nZRUS5Z48ZBW8-gtDl0quO4/edit>  
  A set of criteria about information authority, structure, grammar, and usability.
- <https://legibilidade.com/>  
  Online text readability test, with formulas adapted for Portuguese.
- <https://clarice.ai/>  
  Create original content in a few clicks and review spelling, grammar, and style in one place.

Mais detalhes: <https://nuds.nu.com.br/46f3733aa/p/598527>

### Difference Between Being Transparent and Subjective

We understand that textual repetition — either with the same words, or through explanations using synonymous terms — can be a resource for learning, confirming and fixing information.

However, we recommend avoiding repetitions, redundancies and even sensitive and detailed information about a product or functionality open in the first content layer of a flow.

We suggest reporting fees, rates, conditions, prerequisites about a product or functionality **only in a second layer of content**. Because we want the user's first contact with our content to be attractive and positive.

When it is necessary to inform fees, tariffs and the like in this second layer, we must do it in a simple, direct and less prominent way. Avoid using titles for this purpose; prefer subheadings or part of the content.

If you feel the need, add shortcuts to contextual help (boxes, tooltips) or complementary content, such as FAQs.

#### Tips

- Exercise the power of synthesis.
- Avoid rebusting.
- Avoid content anticipation – wanting to say everything at the same time, without thinking about breaks due to timing (what is essential for that action, at that moment).

---

### Long Texts

Avoid too much text on just one screen. Combine with PD and PM the use of accessory resources to the content such as explanatory images and videos to divide the cognitive load.

If the only resource available is textual:

- Divide the content into paragraphs on the same subject, with a maximum of three lines each.
- Divide the content into simple and direct instructions, separated by bullet points.
- Split content across more than one screen.

Excessive text should also be avoided in specific components. Related to that, read more about truncation and text scaling on the A11Y page.

# Content Patterns (Masterbrand)

Here you will find guidelines defined for the **Masterbrand**.

## Punctuation

Use a **period** in the following cases:

- Subtitles  
- Body text  
- The body of push notifications  
- Helper text  

**Don't** use a period in the following elements:

- Titles  
- Buttons  
- Lists  
- Toast messages  
- Cards in Now Dashboard  

For titles, this follows a well-established editorial convention: a title is seen as an introduction to what follows, not a standalone idea. A period would "close off" the concept prematurely.

**Examples**

- **Do use periods in**: subtitles, embedded errors (like form field errors) and push notifications.  
- **Don't use periods in**: main titles, buttons, form fields, and list items.

## Date and Time

### Date

The goal is complete clarity and understanding. The person cannot be in doubt about the date of the event.

- Avoid using relative words like **"today"** or **"tomorrow"**, which can become confusing and meaningless after a while. Prefer an **exact date**.
- Use the **year** when necessary.

Formats:

- **Running text**: `4 de março` (without leading zero, month in lowercase).  
- **Technical / receipts / statements**: `04 mar.` (with leading zero, abbreviated lowercase month and "." at the end).

**Examples**

- **Do**: abbreviate dates, use a leading zero and "." at the end when it's an interface element (e.g. CC purchases, account statement, investment due date, locked deposit):  
  - `21 jan. 2024`  
  - `21 jan.`  
  - `07 ago.`  

- **Also do**: write the full date in running text or paragraphs, without a leading zero and with the month in lowercase:  
  - `Sua reserva termina em 7 de dezembro de 2024.`  
  - `Você poderá sacar o valor a partir de 7 de dezembro de 2024.`  

- **Don't**: use alternative ways to write dates, such as:  
  - `07 AGO`  
  - `Sua reserva termina em 07 AGO 2024`  
  - `20/08/2024`  

---

### Days of the Week

Days of the week are always written in **lowercase**.

Preferred forms:

- **Full** (standard): e.g. `quarta-feira`  
- **Short** (restricted spaces): e.g. `quarta`  
- **Abbreviation** (very short spaces): e.g. `qua.`  
- **Initial** (charts and calendars): e.g. `q`  

**Examples**

- **Do**: write the days in full when possible:  
  - `O chat funciona de segunda a sexta.`  
  - `O chat funciona sábado e domingo.`  

  When space is restricted, use short forms:  
  - `O chat funciona de seg a sex.`  

- **Don't**:  
  - `O chat funciona de 2a a 6a.`  
  - `O chat funciona Sab e dom.`  

---

### Time

Use the **24-hour system** and **don't put a space** between the number and `h`.

- **Running text**: `9h` (no leading zero, no `:00`);  
- **Technical contexts** (receipts, transaction lists, system logs): `09:21` or `09:21:34` (leading zero and `:`).

**Examples**

- **Do**: use `:` in technical moments, such as transaction lists:  
  - `17:25`  

- **Also do**: use seconds when appropriate, such as account statement or receipt:  
  - `17:25:11`  

- **Also do**: use `h` for running texts:  
  - `9h`  
  - `17h25`  
  - `meio-dia` or `12h`  
  - `meia-noite` or `0h`  

- **Don't**:  
  - Use `h` in technical contexts.  
  - Add a space like `9 h`.

## Numbers and Money

### Quantities

When writing about quantities, **use numerals**, not spelled-out numbers.

**Examples**

- **Do**:  
  - `Você tem 97 ações.`  
  - `Em até 5 dias.`  

- **Don't**:  
  - `Em até cinco dias.`  
  - `Em até 3 dias.`  
  - `Você tem noventa e sete ações.`  

---

### Money

- In **Brazil**, use a **space** between the currency symbol and the amount:  
  - `R$ 25,45`, `R$ 25`  
- In **MX** and **CO**, **don't** use space:  
  - `$3,000`

**Cautions**

- Prefer **not** to use decimals for whole values in most interfaces:  
  - `R$ 25`, `R$ 10 mil`  
  - Avoid `R$ 25,00` or `R$ 10.000,00` in UI copy.  
  - Exception: more technical tables, like a transaction history.

- **Don't**:
  - Forget the currency symbol: `25,55`.  
  - Use leading zeros: `R$ 01`.  
  - Use a plus sign (`+`) for money in.

**Correct Brazilian Real format**

- `R$ 1.500,00`  

Correct formats for other currencies will be added in future versions of this content check tool.

## Fees

### Whole Values

The percent symbol stays **next to** the value, with **no space**. For **integer** values, do not use commas or decimals.

**Examples**

- **Do**:  
  - `25%`  
  - `100%`  

- **Don't**:  
  - `25,00%`  
  - `05%`  
  - `32 %`  
  - `50.000%`  
  - `100,00%`  

---

### Broken Values

Use a **comma** and **only two decimal places**, except when the Legal team explicitly requires more.

**Examples**

- **Do**:  
  - `25,25%`  
  - `99,68%`  

- **Don't**:  
  - `25.25%`  
  - `99.8393830%`  

---

## Line Breaks for Money

To ensure readability, **currency symbol and amount must stay together**.

Guidelines:

- Break text to a **new line** when the amount will not fit on the current one.
- Whenever possible, make the content shorter and more objective.

For technical details on how to preserve this behavior in code, see:  
<https://nuds.nu.com.br/46f3733aa/v/0/p/2335c5-faq/t/5bbb169717>

**Examples**

- **Do**: break content onto a new line if needed, keeping `R$` and the amount on the same line.  
- **Don't**: allow a line break between `R$` and `XX,XX`.

## Lists

People love lists. They:

- Organize information,  
- Make ideas more tangible,  
- Facilitate dynamic reading.

Whenever you need to describe a **step by step** or a **process that has an order**, use **numbered lists**, not bullet points. Do **not** put periods or semicolons at the end of each item.

**Examples of list usage**

- Document lists  
- Benefits a product has  

**Step-by-step example**

1. Open the app  
2. Enter your data  
3. Complete the registration  

---

## Links

Links direct the user to **secondary or supporting information**.

To improve usability:

- Describe what the person will find when clicking, **or**
- Use attention-grabbing phrases like "clique aqui", "saiba mais" when necessary, but always with clear context.

**Examples**

- **Do**:  
  - `Confira os juros detalhados na versão completa do contrato.`  
  - `Clique aqui para conhecer os juros detalhados do contrato.`

## Error Messages

### Basic Formula

> **Problem** (what went wrong) + **Solution** (how to fix it) + **Call to action** (where to go / what to do)

You can combine these elements depending on the purpose:

- Errors and alerts:  
  - Problem + Solution + Call to action  
  - Problem + Call to action  
- Online errors:  
  - Solution  
  - Solution + Call to action  

### Recommendations

- Avoid the **"try again" loop**. Use "try again" only if you or engineering genuinely can't identify a more specific cause.  
- Keep it **simple and straightforward**.  
- Use this moment to **build trust and confidence**.  
- Avoid drama.  
- Provide guidance.  
- Be contextual.

Only **apologize** or **say "please"** if:

- We failed at something we were supposed to deliver, or  
- We made the user do extra work for something that is our fault.

There is also a documentation component in Figma with warning text options and tips on how to choose the best microcopy in each situation.

## Terms and Conditions

### Why we highlight T&Cs

Most people don't read Terms and Conditions.  
At Nubank, **we read them so our customers don't need to.**

- Use the **interface** to highlight the most important items.  
- Use **simple, easy-to-understand language**.  
- Always align suggestions with the **Legal team**.

To decide what to highlight, you can:

- Run research,  
- Look at customer comments on social media,  
- Talk to Xpeers,  
- Connect the highlights to the **product value proposition**.

### Recommendations

- **Make it scannable**
  - Use bullet points, numbers or title/subtitle patterns (List Row component).  

- **Titles**
  - Title must clearly say what the screen is about.  
  - Avoid generic titles.

- **Language**
  - Use simple words that people can understand, not typical legal lingo.

- **Emphasis**
  - Use **bold** for the most important information.

- **Access to the full text**
  - Use a link to redirect customers to the complete Terms and Conditions, or  
  - Show the full text directly on screen via progressive disclosure.

Summarize the most important info and use **progressive disclosure** for the full contract.

### Examples

- **Do**:
  - Specific titles, like `Detalhes do seu acordo`.  
  - Simple wording, like `Órgãos de proteção ao crédito, como o Serasa e Boavista, por exemplo.`  
    - In this example, the T&Cs are organized in a **Q&A structure**, which is recommended when we can adapt and simplify legal information. This can turn the full contract into an easy-to-read version, improving visuals and language.

- **Don't**:
  - Generic titles, like `Informações importantes`.  
  - Legal lingo, like `bureaus de crédito`.

## Emoticons

Emoticons are combinations of punctuation symbols that form a design with meaning, e.g. `:/`, `:)`, `<3`.

At Nubank, **we do not use emoticons**, because their reading and interpretation can be confusing for screen readers used by blind or low-vision people.

## Emojis

### Product

- Avoid using emojis in **in-product flows**.  
- If you choose to use them:
  - Make sure the emoji matches Nu's **tone and voice**.  
  - Check whether the emoji is appropriate for the **moment** in the flow.
- Avoid **pejorative emojis**, which can generate anxiety or insecurity, even as a joke.
- Prefer placing emojis at the **end of sentences**, before the period.  
  - Emojis at the beginning or middle make understanding harder.

### Marketing and Service

- Marketing and Service (chatbot, human attendant) can use emojis more freely to smooth communication and get closer to the target audience.
- Always with **moderation**:
  - Avoid more than **one emoji per paragraph**.
  - Prefer emojis with a **positive** connotation and that respect the Accessibility rules (see `accessible-content.md`). **Emojis to avoid** - 😡 🤮 😱 😭 💩 🔥 👎
